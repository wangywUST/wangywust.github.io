## §1.5　Decision Theory

> 📖 Textbook §1.5 Decision Theory (§1.5.1–§1.5.5)

1. **Inference vs. decision stages**; the joint p(x, t) as the complete summary of uncertainty
2. **Minimizing the misclassification rate** (§1.5.1)
   1. Decision regions and decision boundaries
   2. Optimal rule: assign to the class with the largest posterior p(Cₖ|x)
3. **Minimizing the expected loss** (§1.5.2)
   1. Loss matrix L_kj (cancer-diagnosis example: asymmetric costs)
   2. Optimal rule: minimize Σₖ L_kj p(Cₖ|x)
4. **The reject option** (§1.5.3): threshold θ on the maximum posterior
5. **Inference and decision: three approaches** (§1.5.4)
   1. Generative models (model p(x|Cₖ) and priors) — pros/cons (outlier detection vs. data hunger)
   2. Discriminative models (model p(Cₖ|x) directly)
   3. Discriminant functions (map x directly to a label) — what is lost without posteriors
   4. Why posterior probabilities are useful: rejection, class-prior compensation, combining models, minimizing risk
6. **Loss functions for regression** (§1.5.5)
   1. Squared loss → optimal prediction is the conditional mean E[t|x]
   2. Decomposition of expected squared loss (intrinsic noise term)
   3. Minkowski loss L_q family; q=1 → conditional median, q→0 → conditional mode

## §1.6　Information Theory

> 📖 Textbook §1.6 Information Theory (§1.6.1)

1. **Entropy**
   1. Information content h(x) = −log₂ p(x); entropy H[x] as expected information
   2. Noiseless coding theorem interpretation (entropy as a lower bound on code length)
   3. Discrete entropy maximized by the uniform distribution (Lagrange-multiplier derivation)
2. **Differential entropy**
   1. Definition and its limiting relation to discrete entropy
   2. Maximum-entropy distribution under fixed mean/variance = **Gaussian**
   3. Conditional entropy H[y|x]; chain rule H[x, y] = H[y|x] + H[x]
3. **Relative entropy and mutual information** (§1.6.1)
   1. KL divergence KL(p‖q): definition, non-symmetry, KL ≥ 0 (via Jensen's inequality and convexity)
   2. Data compression interpretation; ML estimation as minimizing KL to the empirical distribution
   3. Mutual information I[x, y] = KL(p(x, y)‖p(x)p(y)); relation to entropies; Bayesian interpretation

## §2.3　The Gaussian Distribution

> 📖 Textbook §2.3 The Gaussian Distribution (§2.3.1–§2.3.9)

1. **Geometry of the multivariate Gaussian**
   1. Mahalanobis distance; eigen-decomposition of Σ; principal-axes ellipsoids
   2. Normalization and moments: E[x] = μ, cov[x] = Σ
   3. Limitations: O(D²) parameters; unimodality → motivates diagonal/isotropic restrictions and mixtures
2. **Conditional Gaussian distributions** (§2.3.1)
   1. Partitioned vectors and precision matrix Λ = Σ⁻¹
   2. p(x_a|x_b) is Gaussian; conditional mean linear in x_b; conditional covariance via Schur complement
3. **Marginal Gaussian distributions** (§2.3.2): p(x_a) Gaussian; results summarized in the partitioned-Gaussian theorem
4. **Bayes' theorem for Gaussian variables** (§2.3.3)
   1. Linear-Gaussian model: p(x) and p(y|x) Gaussian with linear mean
   2. Closed-form marginal p(y) and posterior p(x|y) (the workhorse identity reused in Ch.3, Ch.12, Ch.13)
5. **Maximum likelihood for the Gaussian** (§2.3.4): sufficient statistics; μ_ML, Σ_ML; bias correction of covariance
6. **Sequential estimation** (§2.3.5)
   1. Recursive update of μ_ML
   2. **Robbins–Monro algorithm** for general sequential ML root-finding; conditions on step sizes
7. **Bayesian inference for the Gaussian** (§2.3.6)
   1. Known variance, unknown mean: Gaussian conjugate prior; precision addition
   2. Known mean, unknown precision: **Gamma** conjugate prior; effective observations
   3. Unknown mean and precision: **Gaussian-Gamma** joint conjugate prior; multivariate case → **Gaussian-Wishart**
8. **Student's t-distribution** (§2.3.7)
   1. Derivation: infinite mixture of Gaussians with Gamma-distributed precision
   2. Heavy tails and **robustness** to outliers (comparison experiment vs. Gaussian ML)
   3. Multivariate t; parameters ν, μ, Λ
9. **Periodic variables** (§2.3.8) *(introduction only; trim first if hours are limited)*
   1. Failure of the ordinary Gaussian for angles; mean direction via unit vectors
   2. **von Mises distribution**: derivation by conditioning a 2-D Gaussian on the unit circle; concentration parameter m; ML solutions; Bessel function I₀
10. **Mixtures of Gaussians** (§2.3.9)
    1. Superposition Σπₖ N(x|μₖ, Σₖ); mixing coefficients as probabilities
    2. Responsibilities γₖ(x) via Bayes' theorem (preview of EM, lecture Chapter 9)
    3. Log-likelihood: no closed-form ML → iterative optimization needed

## §2.4　The Exponential Family

> 📖 Textbook §2.4 The Exponential Family (§2.4.1–§2.4.3)

1. **General form** p(x|η) = h(x)g(η)exp{ηᵀu(x)}
   1. Bernoulli as exponential family: logistic sigmoid emerges as the link
   2. Multinomial: softmax function emerges; degrees-of-freedom handling
   3. Gaussian: natural parameters and sufficient statistics
2. **Maximum likelihood and sufficient statistics** (§2.4.1)
   1. −∇ln g(η) = E[u(x)]; moments from derivatives of the log-partition
   2. ML depends on data only through Σu(xₙ) (sufficiency)
3. **Conjugate priors** (§2.4.2): general conjugate form for exponential-family likelihoods; χ, ν as pseudo-observations
4. **Noninformative priors** (§2.4.3)
   1. Location parameters → flat prior; scale parameters → 1/σ prior
   2. Improper priors and their practical treatment

## §2.5　Nonparametric Methods

> 📖 Textbook §2.5 Nonparametric Methods (§2.5.1–§2.5.2)

1. **Histogram density estimation**: bin width as smoothing parameter; discontinuities; curse of dimensionality
2. **General local framework**: p(x) ≈ K/(NV); fix V (kernel) vs. fix K (nearest neighbours)
3. **Kernel density estimators** (§2.5.1)
   1. Parzen window (hypercube kernel); smooth Gaussian kernels
   2. Bandwidth h: bias–variance trade-off in the smoothing parameter
4. **Nearest-neighbour methods** (§2.5.2)
   1. K-NN density estimation (fix K, grow the sphere)
   2. **K-NN classification** from class-conditional densities; the K = 1 rule
   3. Asymptotic result: 1-NN error ≤ 2 × Bayes error (N → ∞)
   4. Drawbacks: storage of the whole training set; motivates parametric/latent models

## §4.1　Discriminant Functions

> 📖 Textbook §4.1 Discriminant Functions (§4.1.1–§4.1.7)
